1. "Your topology ordering is none > ring > star > random > FC — but
   that's just saying more connectivity = faster convergence. Isn't
   this already well-known from island model literature?"

   ANSWER: The qualitative observation that connectivity affects
   convergence is known, yes — Whitley and Skolicki showed this for
   individual domains. What's new is three things: (a) the ordering
   is *identical* across six unrelated domains with perfect rank
   concordance W=1.0, which has never been demonstrated; (b) the
   spectral theory predicts the ring/star inversion at n=7 islands,
   which we confirm with p=6.6×10⁻⁵ — that's a falsifiable
   quantitative prediction, not just "more connectivity = less
   diversity"; and (c) framing topology as a compositional property
   rather than a tuning parameter is what lets us ask *why* the
   ordering is domain-independent, not just observe that it is.

2. "You use 10 seeds for fingerprints and only 3 domains. How do you
   know the fingerprint shapes aren't artifacts of your specific
   parameter choices?"

   ANSWER: The fingerprint patterns are qualitatively distinct — an
   85% crash followed by recovery (hourglass) vs monotonic decline
   (flat) vs irreversible collapse to 0.02 (adaptive). These aren't
   subtle statistical effects that more seeds would clarify; they're
   visually unambiguous trajectory shapes that hold across three
   different genome types (60-bit, 40-bit, 50-bit) and three
   different fitness landscapes. That said, you're right that we
   haven't varied the phase boundaries or operator parameters within
   each strategy — showing robustness to those variations is future
   work. The key claim is that the *composition pattern* determines
   the *qualitative shape*, not the exact numbers.

3. "What about fitness? You show diversity dynamics are
   composition-determined, but does the best-fitness strategy also
   follow from composition? Or does the diversity advantage of island
   models come at a fitness cost?"

   ANSWER: Honest answer — we didn't measure best fitness in this
   paper, and that's deliberate. We're making a structural claim
   about diversity dynamics, not an optimization claim. The
   diversity-fitness relationship is complex and domain-dependent:
   in co-evolutionary domains like No Thanks!, maintaining diversity
   *is* maintaining fitness because the landscape shifts. In
   unimodal domains like OneMax, you actually want diversity to
   collapse. The right question isn't "which strategy finds the best
   solution?" but "which composition gives you the diversity
   trajectory you need for your problem?" — and fingerprints are the
   diagnostic tool for that.

4. "The sorting network domain violates your ordering. How do you
   characterize which domains will satisfy the invariant and which
   won't? Can you predict it in advance?"

   ANSWER: In sorting networks, all topologies converge identically
   — the domain imposes such uniform convergence pressure that
   topology effects vanish. The scope condition is: the invariant
   holds when the fitness landscape admits enough selective gradient
   that *different islands can maintain different genetic material*.
   When a domain's structure forces all populations toward the same
   genotype regardless of isolation, topology has nothing to work
   with. We can't give you a crisp a priori test yet — that's an
   open question. But the fact that the ordering holds for
   co-evolutionary domains with no fixed landscape (No Thanks!,
   checkers) suggests the scope is broad. Sorting networks appear
   to be the exception, not the rule.

6. "The Kleisli composition is elegant, but does it give you anything
   you couldn't get with plain function composition in any language?
   What experiment could you run with the categorical framework that
   you couldn't run without it?"

   ANSWER: The formalism generated the hypotheses, not the other
   way around. When we translated the Rust GA into Haskell, the
   Kleisli structure revealed that strategies like "island model"
   and "hourglass" are *the same kind of thing* — compositions of
   the same base arrows. That's what led us to ask: if they're the
   same kind of thing composed differently, do the compositions have
   characteristic signatures? The fingerprint experiments came
   directly from that question. You *could* run the same experiments
   without the framework — but you wouldn't think to ask the
   question. The framework's value is conceptual: it tells you
   what's invariant (the composition mechanism) and what varies
   (the objects being composed), which guides where to look for
   universal properties.

7. "The λ₂ spectral gap explanation is compelling. Have you tested
   non-standard topologies — small-world, scale-free — to see if λ₂
   fully predicts the diversity ordering?"

   ANSWER: Not yet — we tested ring, star, random Erdős-Rényi, and
   fully connected. Small-world and scale-free topologies are
   natural next steps. The random topology result is actually
   interesting here: its snapshot λ₂ is low but its effective
   diversity erosion is high, which suggests that for time-varying
   topologies, the instantaneous λ₂ isn't the whole story — you
   need the time-averaged spectral properties. For fixed topologies,
   λ₂ predicts the ordering perfectly across our test set, but we
   haven't tested enough topologies to rule out confounds with
   other graph properties like diameter or expansion.

8. "Your adaptive strategy collapses to 0.02 diversity. Is that a
   bug or a feature? In practice, adaptive GAs balance exploration
   and exploitation — yours seems pathological."

   ANSWER: It's neither a bug nor a strawman — it's what happens
   when you compose an exploration phase with a focus phase that has
   no diversity-preservation mechanism. The plateau detector triggers
   the switch when fitness stagnates for 5 generations, and the
   focus phase uses stronger selection (tournament size 5 vs 3) with
   no compensating mutation increase. A more sophisticated adaptive
   strategy might maintain a diversity floor — but that would be a
   *different composition*, and it would produce a *different
   fingerprint*. That's exactly the point: the fingerprint tells you
   what your composition is actually doing to diversity, so you can
   diagnose when "adaptive" means "collapse."

9. "You mention the island construction is a functor. Can you say
   more about what kind of functor, and what the coherence conditions
   are?"

   ⚠️  DANGER ZONE — this leads directly into the ACT paper.

   ANSWER: The short version is that when there's no migration, the
   island construction preserves composition exactly — it's strict.
   When you add migration, it doesn't preserve composition exactly
   because migrating after each step is different from migrating
   after the composed steps. The discrepancy is what drives the
   diversity ordering. We're working on the formal construction —
   the coherence conditions and the explicit characterisation of
   that discrepancy are the subject of a companion paper under
   review at another venue.

10. "How does this relate to multi-agent LLM systems? The related
    work mentions AlphaEvolve and topology effects in multi-agent
    systems."

    ⚠️  DANGER ZONE — this leads toward the EUMAS paper.

    ANSWER: The related work connection is that several groups have
    independently found that topology matters for multi-agent LLM
    systems — Yang et al. found up to 10% performance variation
    across fixed topologies, and Kim et al. found 17.2× error
    amplification for independent vs 4.4× for centralized
    compositions. Our results strengthen this by showing that in the
    GA setting the topology ordering is identical across six unrelated
    domains — the composition, not the task, is what fixes it.
    Whether the same universal ordering holds for LLM agent
    topologies is an interesting open question — we'd expect the
    compositional structure to transfer, but we haven't tested it.

11. "The No Thanks! result — co-evolutionary with no fixed fitness
    landscape, yet W=1.0. Isn't that surprising? What's the
    mechanism?"

    ★ THIS IS YOUR BEST QUESTION — lean into it.

    ANSWER: Yes, it's the result we're most proud of. In No Thanks!,
    fitness is entirely relative — your strategy's fitness depends on
    who you're playing against, and that changes every generation.
    There is literally no fixed landscape. Yet the topology ordering
    holds with ρ=1.0, and the phase transition (none→ring diversity
    drop) is 53.9% — the largest of any domain. The mechanism is
    that topology governs *genetic mixing rate*, not *fitness
    convergence*. Whether fitness is fixed or co-evolutionary, more
    connected topologies homogenise genomes faster because they
    physically move more genetic material between subpopulations.
    The diversity ordering is about information flow in the migration
    graph, not about the fitness landscape at all — which is exactly
    what the compositional framework predicts. Migration is a
    property of the composition, not of the fitness function.

12. "You say composition structure governs diversity dynamics. But
    surely population size, mutation rate, and selection pressure
    also matter enormously. Aren't you overstating the claim?"

    ANSWER: You're right that they matter, and I want to be precise
    about what we did and didn't hold fixed. In the *fingerprint*
    experiments the operators are the same functions — same tournament
    selection, same one-point crossover, same bit-flip mutation — but
    the composition specifies the *schedule*: which parameterisation
    of each operator runs in which phase. Hourglass really does raise
    mutation in its explore and diversify phases, and its tournament
    size moves between 2 and 5. That is what an "explore phase" means.
    So the claim isn't that parameters don't matter — and I'll go
    further. That experiment cannot separate the arrangement from the
    mutation rate. The terminal mutation rate alone accounts for almost
    all of the spread. So we are retiring the variance number we had
    from it. What survives is the *shapes* — spike, crash, rebound —
    across three genome lengths and three landscapes, with an
    eighteen-fold spread in final diversity. For the composition claim
    itself, look at the topology experiment instead: there every
    operator and every parameter is held identical, and the only thing
    that changes is the migration graph — and the ordering is still
    identical across six domains, with topology explaining sixty-eight
    percent of the within-domain variance in final diversity.

    NOTE FOR LYRA: the old answer to this question claimed "same
    mutation rate, same tournament size." That was FALSE (see
    qa-corrections.md, section D) and it contradicted Q8 above. Do not
    assert that parameters were held constant in the fingerprint
