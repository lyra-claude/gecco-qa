# Q&A corrections — read before the session

These came out of an adversarial red-team of the camera-ready, independently verified by reproducing every number from our own supplementary CSVs. The core result — the six-domain ordering, Kendall's W = 1.0, p = 0.00008 — reproduces exactly as published and is not in question. What follows are two overstated supporting statistics and one prep note that is factually wrong. All three are survivable if we know about them going in. Each section gives the problem, the real numbers, and a drafted answer.

## 1. The 23.9x variance statistic (critical)

- What the paper says: abstract, contributions, Table 2 caption, related work, conclusion — "topology explains 23.9x more variance than domain," described as a two-way ANOVA on diversity dynamics.
- What it actually is: `supplementary/experiments/multi_domain_analysis.py`, `compute_variance_decomposition(all_onset_results)` decomposes **coupling-onset timing** (a generation number derived from `1.0 - population_divergence`), iterating over `COUPLED_TOPOS = ['ring','star','random','fully_connected']` — **'none' is excluded**, and 'none' is the top element of our ordering.
- Forensics (all independently reproduced): the string "ANOVA" appears in zero supplementary scripts — no F-test is computed anywhere. Our reported p_domain = 0.945 matches the ONSET Kruskal-Wallis (0.955); a real diversity ANOVA gives p_domain < 1e-200. Our reported F_domain = 0.13 at df=5 implies p ~ 0.985, not 0.945 (p=0.945 corresponds to df~3 — the df of the four coupled topologies). And 23.9x is not reproducible from any subset of domains: sweeping all 57 combinations of 2-6 domains, the 6-domain onset value is 57.8x.
- The honest ANOVA (final-gen `hamming_diversity`, 6 domains x 5 topologies, N=760): **domain 62.1% of variance (F=580), topology 21.8% (F=255)**. Domain explains ~2.8x MORE than topology. The direction of the headline claim reverses.
- DRAFTED ANSWER: "The 23.9x is a decomposition of coupling-onset *timing* across the four coupled topologies — how long after migration starts the topology effect appears. It is not a decomposition of diversity levels, and the abstract's phrasing is looser than it should be. Absolute diversity levels obviously differ by domain: a 20-bit OneMax genome and a 64-bit checkers genome have different diversity scales. The domain-invariance claim is about the *ordering* — which is what Kendall's W tests — and about onset timing. Run on levels, domain dominates, and it should."
- **DO NOT** defend p_domain = 0.945 as evidence that domain doesn't affect diversity. It is a p-value from a different response variable, and it is also an attempt to accept a null hypothesis. Both are indefensible.

## 2. The ordering is a between-island (F_ST) result (serious)

- `hamming_diversity` is pooled across all 5 islands. With 5 islands of 16, ~81% of pairs in that metric are between-island pairs (2560 of 3160). The per-island columns (`island_0..4_diversity`) ship in our own supplementary.
- Within-island diversity at final generation: **'none' is LAST in 5 of 6 domains** (maze, graph_coloring, knapsack, nothanks, checkers) and never 1st. **Kendall's W falls from 1.0 to 0.461** (chi2=11.07, p=0.026). The within-island ordering is roughly random > ring > fully_connected > star > none — a near-inversion.
- Mechanism: migration converts between-deme variance into within-deme variance. This is Wright's island model; implied F_ST runs 0.77 (none) -> 0.35 (FC) in maze.
- DRAFTED ANSWER: "It's the metapopulation-pooled metric, and you're right about what that implies: migration converts between-island variance into within-island variance. Both are in our data — within-island diversity actually *increases* with connectivity, exactly as Wright's island model has predicted since 1931. What we measure is the total genotypic spread of the metapopulation, and that's monotone in the mixing rate of the migration graph. Our result is consistent with classical population genetics — that's reassurance, not a problem. What's new is that the *ordering over topologies* is identical across six unrelated domains including co-evolutionary ones, and that it tracks a spectral property of the composition. But you're right that we should say 'metapopulation diversity' and we should cite F_ST. That's a fair hit."
- Conceding this beats being caught by it. The per-island columns are in the public supplement; anyone with pandas finds this in ten minutes.

## 3. questions.txt Q12 is factually false (fix before the session)

- Q12 currently answers: "We held those parameters constant across all experiments — same population size, same mutation rate, same tournament size."
- `strategy_fingerprints.py` says otherwise. flat: tournament 3, mut 1/L. **hourglass: tournament 2/5/2, mutation 2x / 0.5x / 2x base.** **adaptive: tournament 2 -> 5, mutation 2x -> 0.5x.** island: tournament 3, mut 1/L. Mutation rate spans 4x and tournament size spans 2-5 across strategies. Only crossover rate (0.8) is genuinely constant.
- It also contradicts our own Q8, which already concedes "the focus phase uses stronger selection (tournament size 5 vs 3)". If Q8 and Q12 are both asked, we contradict ourselves on stage.
- Q12 additionally repeats the 23.9x-as-diversity gloss, scripting problem #1 for the stage.
- REPLACEMENT ANSWER: "The operators are the same functions — same tournament selection, same one-point crossover, same bit-flip mutation. What the composition specifies is the *schedule*: which parameterisation of each operator runs in which phase. So yes, hourglass raises mutation in its explore and diversify phases — that is what an 'explore phase' means. The claim isn't that parameters don't matter; it's that the arrangement produces a *shape* — spike, crash, rebound — that's invariant across three genome lengths and three landscapes. If you want the clean version of the claim, look at the topology experiment instead: there the operators *and* every parameter are held identical, and the only thing that changes is the migration graph — and the ordering is still identical across six domains."
- Note: the paper's sentence "All four strategies use identical selection, crossover, and mutation operators" (Sec. 5) is defensible only if "operators" means the functions, not their parameterisations. Do not assert that parameters were held constant.

## 4. Ring vs star — prep, not a correction

- The lambda_2 rule (smaller lambda_2 -> higher diversity) points the wrong way at n=5: lambda_2(ring)=1.382 > lambda_2(star)=1.0 predicts star more diverse, but we report ring > star. The paper already addresses this (line 388): it declares the two indistinguishable at n=5 (Fisher's p=0.14) and stakes the mechanism on the n=7 reversal, which genuinely validates. No correction needed.
- The residual tension worth prepping: **W = 1.0 requires ring > star in all six domains**, while the n=5 lambda_2 rescue requires them to be *indistinguishable*. Ring does beat star 6/6 (sign test p=0.031). We can't fully bank on both.
- DRAFTED ANSWER: "That's the sharpest question you could ask, and ring/star is the weak link. lambda_2 cleanly separates 'none' from the coupled topologies and the sparse from the dense ones, but it does not resolve ring vs star at n=5 — the raw inequality points the wrong way there. What I think is happening: lambda_2 governs the *asymptotic* consensus rate, while diversity at 100 generations is a *transient*. Rings support isolation-by-distance — material propagates only locally, so structure persists — whereas a star hub reaches every island in one hop. That's a mechanism lambda_2 alone doesn't capture. So: lambda_2 is a good first-order predictor with a known failure mode, and I'd rather say that than overclaim."

## What holds up

State plainly: the six-domain ordering (none > ring > star > random > fully_connected), Kendall's W = 1.0 at p = 0.00008, and the n=7 confirmatory prediction all reproduce exactly as published. Independently re-derived from the raw CSVs this session. The sorting-networks counterexample is honestly reported in the paper. This is not a broken paper — it is a paper with two overstated supporting statistics and one bad prep note.
